# Molecular Mechanisms Driving Chronic RPE Inflammation and Sub-RPE Drusen Biogenesis in Dry Age-Related Macular Degeneration

## Overview

Dry age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a complex, multifactorial disease in which chronic inflammation, lysosomal failure, and toxic aggregate accumulation converge on retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells, driving progressive sub-RPE drusen formation and eventual geographic atrophy. The molecular pathogenesis involves tightly interconnected cascades—complement dysregulation, lysosomal dysfunction, lipid-rich protein aggregate buildup, and multiple cell death programs—that reinforce one another in a self-amplifying vicious cycle. The following sections detail each mechanism and their interactions based on current literature.

## 1. Dysregulation of the Complement Cascade

### 1.1 Alternative Pathway Amplification and CFH Dysfunction

The complement system, particularly the alternative pathway, is central to AMD pathogenesis. Genome-wide association studies have positioned complement dysregulation at the core of AMD research, with the CFH Y402H polymorphism being the single strongest genetic risk factor (kauppinen2016inflammationandits pages 7-8, skerka2026anewperspective pages 1-2). Under homeostatic conditions, complement factor H (CFH) restrains the alternative pathway by competing with complement factor B (CFB) for C3b binding, thereby preventing persistent C3 convertase activity (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 9-11, garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 11-12). The Y402H variant, located in the SCR-6/8 domain, weakens CFH binding to both C-reactive protein (CRP) and malondialdehyde (MDA), a lipid peroxidation product (kauppinen2016inflammationandits pages 7-8). This impaired binding has several consequences: RPE-choroid cells in Y402H homozygous individuals are less protected from elevated CRP levels, as demonstrated by the approximately 2.5-fold higher CRP deposition in the choroid of 402HH individuals compared to 402YY individuals (johnson2006individualshomozygousfor pages 1-2, johnson2006individualshomozygousfor pages 2-4). Additionally, the Y402H variant fails to generate anti-inflammatory iC3b on MDA-loaded surfaces, further compromising complement regulation (kauppinen2016inflammationandits pages 7-8). The Y402H polymorphism also specifically reduces Factor H's binding affinity for oxidized low-density lipoproteins and MDA-modified proteins on damaged cellular surfaces, impairing clearance of oxidized lipids by RPE cells and causing intracellular lipid accumulation (skerka2026anewperspective pages 8-9).

### 1.2 Complement Activation in Drusen and the Sub-RPE Space

Drusen accumulate all complement components including regulatory proteins (CFH), activation products (C3, C3a, C3b, C5a), and membrane attack complex (MAC) components (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 5-7). Classical pathway activation occurs through C1q binding to apolipoprotein E-rich deposits and amyloid-beta within drusen (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 5-7). The anaphylatoxins C3a and C5a act as potent chemotactic factors that recruit neutrophils and monocytes, polarize microglia toward the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype, and activate inflammasome signaling (kauppinen2016inflammationandits pages 7-8). Opsonins (C3b, iC3b, C4b) can target stressed RPE cells and photoreceptors for phagocytosis, contributing to bystander tissue damage (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 11-12). The terminal complement pathway generates MAC (C5b-9), which creates transmembrane pores causing cell lysis; sublytic MAC additionally activates NLRP3 inflammasome through calcium-dependent mechanisms (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 9-11, gao2017nlrp3inflammasomeactivity pages 30-35). Furthermore, oxidative stress in RPE cells reduces surface expression of complement inhibitors CD46, CD55, and CD59, permitting unregulated complement activation on RPE surfaces (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 5-7).

### 1.3 Age-Related Shift in Complement Balance

With aging, complement activation increases while regulatory mechanisms become progressively inadequate, predisposing the RPE-Bruch's membrane-choriocapillaris complex to complement-mediated inflammation (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 2-3). Age-related upregulation of complement factors C3, CFB, and CFH occurs in RPE cells, but the protective capacity of these regulators is increasingly overwhelmed by accumulating damage signals (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 2-3). The complement system thus functions as a dual-edged sword: while necessary for normal retinal maintenance and debris clearance, its unrestricted activation following chronic injury directly damages retinal tissue and recruits inflammatory cells, causing bystander destruction of surrounding cells (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 3-4).

## 2. Lysosomal Dysfunction and Impaired Autophagy

### 2.1 Age-Related Decline in Lysosomal Capacity

RPE cells are among the most metabolically active phagocytes in the body, daily internalizing and digesting large volumes of spent photoreceptor outer segments (POS). This process requires highly functional lysosomes and intact autophagy machinery (kinnunen2012molecularmechanismsof pages 1-2). With aging, lysosomal enzyme activity declines, autophagy flux diminishes, and the capacity to clear damaged organelles and misfolded proteins deteriorates (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5). This decline shifts RPE cells toward a senescent phenotype characterized by lipofuscin accumulation, altered autophagy activity, and a pro-inflammatory senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) that includes ROS, cytokines, chemokines, and proteases (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5).

### 2.2 Lysosomal Membrane Permeabilization and Cathepsin Dysfunction

A critical finding in AMD pathobiology is that oxidative stress induces lysosomal membrane permeabilization (LMP) in RPE cells. Iron overload, which catalyzes reactive oxygen species formation via the Fenton reaction, produces lipid peroxidation products including 4-hydroxynonenal (4-HNE), carboxyethylpyrrole (CEP), and MDA that directly inhibit lysosomal enzyme activity (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 1-3). Cathepsin D, the principal lysosomal protease in RPE, shows impaired maturation under these conditions, with a 6-fold increase in the pro-cathepsin D to mature cathepsin D ratio (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 16-18). Acid lipase and acid sphingomyelinase activities are selectively reduced due to iron-induced damage to histidine and cysteine residues in these enzymes (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 22-25). Galectin-3 translocation to damaged lysosomal membranes serves as a marker of cytotoxic LMP, with strong galectin-3 immunolabel colocalizing with LAMP1 in iron-overloaded RPE (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 16-18). The consequences of LMP include cytosolic cathepsin leakage, activation of pro-apoptotic proteins Bid and Bax, depletion of caspase inhibitor XIAP, and ultimately mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization and cell death (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 22-25).

### 2.3 Ceramide Accumulation and Lipid Metabolic Disruption

Iron-induced lysosomal dysfunction leads to dramatic ceramide accumulation in RPE cells, with ceramide levels elevated 4–20-fold depending on fatty acid chain length, and dihydroceramides and lactosylceramides showing 4–14 and 6–14-fold increases, respectively (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 18-20). This results from upregulation of sphingomyelinase and glucosylceramidase enzymes combined with impaired ceramide clearance (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 20-22). Excess cholesterol in aging RPE lysosomes traps the anionic lipid bis(monoacylglycero)phosphate (BMP), further disrupting lysosomal enzyme function and lipid handling (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 5-6).

### 2.4 Secretory Autophagy and Drusen Biogenesis

When degradative autophagy fails, RPE cells engage secretory autophagy as a compensatory mechanism. This unconventional secretion pathway delivers cargo including IL-1β, IL-18, amyloid-β, and HMGB1 via a TRIM16-SNARE system involving SEC22B, STX proteins, and SNAP proteins (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 5-8). Senescent RPE cells with disturbed autophagy activity and a pro-inflammatory secretome are proposed to regulate drusen biogenesis and advanced AMD development (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5). Autophagic Secretion of Mitochondria (ASM), a related secretory pathway, may extrude damaged mitochondria that contribute to the organic material found in sub-RPE deposits (blasiak2024potentialofautophagy pages 13-14). The net result is that impaired intracellular degradation diverts undigested cargo and inflammatory mediators toward the basal RPE surface and into the sub-RPE space, directly contributing to drusen growth (blasiak2024potentialofautophagy pages 13-14, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 1-2).

## 3. Accumulation of Lipid-Rich Protein Aggregates

### 3.1 Lipofuscin and A2E Toxicity

Lipofuscin is a heterogeneous, autofluorescent lipid-protein aggregate that accumulates progressively in RPE lysosomes, eventually occupying up to 20% of the aged RPE cytoplasm (hussain2025extracellularmatrix(ecm) pages 5-6). It is composed primarily of vitamin A-derived fluorophores, particularly the bisretinoid A2E, which arises from the non-enzymatic reaction of retinal with ethanolamine during POS processing (kinnunen2012molecularmechanismsof pages 1-2). A2E exerts multiple toxic effects: it inhibits lysosomal proton pumps and elevates pH, disrupts membrane integrity, inhibits proteasomal and lysosomal degradation of oxidized proteins, acts as a photosensitizer generating ROS upon blue light exposure, and induces telomeric shortening and DNA damage leading to RPE senescence (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 5-6, fanjulmoles2016relationshipbetweenoxidative pages 7-8, hussain2025extracellularmatrix(ecm) pages 5-6). A2E also directly activates NLRP3 inflammasome by causing lysosomal damage and cathepsin release into the cytoplasm, linking lipofuscin accumulation to inflammatory cell death (chen2022spotlightonpyroptosis pages 9-12).

### 3.2 RPE-Derived Lipoproteins and Drusen Composition

Drusen are composed of over 40% lipids, predominantly esterified and unesterified cholesterol and phosphatidylcholine, derived from large apolipoprotein B,E-containing lipoproteins constitutively secreted by the RPE (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 4-5, iliescu2024nonneovascularagerelatedmacular pages 2-3). Proteomic analysis has identified approximately 1,062 proteins in drusen, including complement components (with C3 and C4 significantly elevated in AMD), TIMP3, clusterin, vitronectin, serum albumin, and crystallins (crabb2014theproteomicsof pages 5-6, crabb2014theproteomicsof pages 1-2). Oxidative modification of these deposits generates lipoxidation products, particularly carboxyethylpyrrole adducts from DHA-containing lipid oxidation, which are significantly more abundant in AMD tissues (crabb2014theproteomicsof pages 8-9). The aging RPE's declining capacity to metabolize lipids is a critical factor in drusen generation, as impaired lipid processing allows progressive sub-RPE deposit accumulation (iliescu2024nonneovascularagerelatedmacular pages 2-3, garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 4-5).

### 3.3 Oxidized Lipoproteins as Complement Activators

The lipid-rich deposits in the sub-RPE space serve as nucleation points for complement deposition and chronic immune activation. ApoE-rich serum proteins in sub-RPE deposits activate the complement cascade and promote complement complex deposition (crabb2014theproteomicsof pages 8-9). Amyloid-beta, also found in drusen, further contributes to complement activation and NLRP3 inflammasome engagement (kawa2014complementsystemin pages 5-7, chen2022spotlightonpyroptosis pages 3-4). The "lipid wall" formed by basal linear deposits impairs nutrient and oxygen transfer across Bruch's membrane to the RPE, creating a hypoxic and metabolically stressed microenvironment that exacerbates RPE dysfunction (wong2022exploringthepathogenesis pages 8-9).

## 4. Cell Death Pathways

### 4.1 Pyroptosis via NLRP3 Inflammasome

Pyroptosis, an inflammatory form of programmed cell death, is increasingly recognized as a predominant mechanism of RPE death in AMD. NLRP3 inflammasome activation follows a two-signal model: Signal 1 involves NF-κB-dependent priming that induces NLRP3 and pro-IL-1β expression; Signal 2 involves lysosomal destabilization causing cathepsin B and L leakage into the cytosol, which mediates NLRP3 inflammasome assembly (tseng2013nlrp3inflammasomeactivation pages 9-10). Activated caspase-1 then cleaves pro-IL-1β and pro-IL-18 into mature cytokines and cleaves gasdermin D (GSDMD), whose N-terminal domain forms pores in the plasma membrane, causing cell swelling and lytic rupture characteristic of pyroptosis (chen2022spotlightonpyroptosis pages 12-13). Drusen components including amyloid-β, complement factor C1q, C3a, C5a, sublytic MAC, and A2E all serve as activation signals for this pathway (tseng2013nlrp3inflammasomeactivation pages 9-10, chen2022spotlightonpyroptosis pages 9-12, gao2017nlrp3inflammasomeactivity pages 30-35). NLRP3 upregulation has been demonstrated in the RPE of eyes affected by both geographic atrophy and neovascular AMD (tseng2013nlrp3inflammasomeactivation pages 1-2). Blocking lysosomal disruption or inhibiting caspase-1 abrogates both IL-1β release and RPE cytotoxicity, confirming the mechanistic link between lysosomal dysfunction, inflammasome activation, and pyroptotic cell death (tseng2013nlrp3inflammasomeactivation pages 7-9).

### 4.2 Apoptosis and Lysosomal-Mitochondrial Cross-Talk

Lysosomal membrane permeabilization also triggers apoptotic pathways through cytosolic cathepsin-mediated activation of Bid and Bax, which lead to mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization, depletion of the caspase inhibitor XIAP, and execution of intrinsic apoptosis (zhang2023oxidativestressinduces pages 22-25). A2E-mediated RPE damage specifically induces blue light-dependent apoptosis through membrane disruption and lysosomal degradation inhibition (fanjulmoles2016relationshipbetweenoxidative pages 7-8). The p62/SQSTM1 protein serves as a critical regulatory node, modulating cell fate through the TNF receptor-associated factor 6-NF-κB survival pathway and caspase-8 activation, which can trigger death signaling complexes (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 8-9).

### 4.3 Necroptosis and Additional Death Programs

Ultrastructural pathology and inflammatory signaling studies suggest that necroptosis-like inflammatory death contributes to RPE degeneration in some AMD settings, particularly where energy depletion combines with oxidative stress (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 4-5). Poly(ADP-ribosylation), normally a homeostatic DNA repair process, can convert to a cell death pathway (parthanatos) under conditions of severe oxidative damage and energy crisis (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 4-5). Current evidence indicates that multiple cell death programs operate simultaneously rather than through a single exclusive mechanism, with the predominant pathway likely varying by disease stage and local microenvironmental conditions.

## 5. The Vicious Cycle of Inflammation and Drusen Growth

The pathogenic mechanisms described above are not isolated but form self-reinforcing feedback loops. Impaired lysosomal degradation leads to lipofuscin accumulation and secretory autophagy-mediated extrusion of undegraded cargo, contributing to drusen formation (blasiak2024potentialofautophagy pages 13-14, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 5-8). Drusen deposits activate complement through multiple pathways and serve as inflammatory foci that recruit and activate myeloid cells (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 9-11, kawa2014complementsystemin pages 5-7). Complement activation generates anaphylatoxins and MAC that further damage RPE cells, amplify inflammation, and prime NLRP3 inflammasome (garciagarcia2022pathophysiologyofagerelated pages 11-12, gao2017nlrp3inflammasomeactivity pages 30-35). Inflammasome-mediated pyroptosis releases additional DAMPs and inflammatory mediators that perpetuate complement activation and immune cell recruitment (tseng2013nlrp3inflammasomeactivation pages 9-10, chen2022spotlightonpyroptosis pages 12-13). RPE cell death further reduces the clearance capacity for sub-RPE deposits, accelerating drusen growth and geographic atrophy expansion. This self-amplifying cycle of oxidative damage, lysosomal failure, toxic aggregate accumulation, complement dysregulation, and inflammatory cell death constitutes the molecular basis for the chronically progressive nature of dry AMD.

## Conclusion

The pathogenesis of dry AMD involves a convergent set of molecular mechanisms in which complement cascade dysregulation, lysosomal dysfunction, lipid-rich protein aggregate accumulation, and inflammatory cell death pathways are inextricably linked. The CFH Y402H polymorphism and related complement gene variants establish a genetic predisposition to impaired complement regulation, while age-related decline in lysosomal and autophagic capacity sets the stage for toxic aggregate buildup. Lipofuscin, A2E, oxidized lipoproteins, and amyloid-beta serve dual roles as both products of failed cellular clearance and triggers of complement activation and inflammasome engagement. The resulting NLRP3-driven pyroptosis and related cell death pathways release inflammatory mediators that sustain chronic para-inflammation, perpetuating a vicious cycle of RPE degeneration and drusen biogenesis that ultimately leads to geographic atrophy and vision loss.

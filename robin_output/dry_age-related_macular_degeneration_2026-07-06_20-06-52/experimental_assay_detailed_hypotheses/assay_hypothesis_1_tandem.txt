## Evaluation of the Tandem Reporter Autophagic Flux Assay for Testing Therapeutics for Dry Age-Related Macular Degeneration

### Assay Overview

The Tandem Reporter Autophagic Flux Assay employs a GFP-mCherry-LC3 (or mRFP-GFP-LC3) fusion construct to provide real-time, spatially resolved quantification of autophagic flux in living cells. The assay models a central aspect of dry AMD pathogenesis: the dysfunction of the autophagy-lysosome degradation pathway in retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells. The reporter exploits the differential pH sensitivity of GFP and mCherry/RFP fluorophores. In the neutral pH of the cytoplasm and nascent autophagosomes, both fluorophores emit light, producing yellow puncta when images are merged. Upon autophagosome-lysosome fusion, the acidic autolysosomal environment (pH ~4.5–5.0) quenches GFP fluorescence while mCherry/RFP remains stable, yielding red-only puncta (mizushima2020autophagyassaysfor pages 5-7, toit2018measuringautophagosomeflux pages 13-16, barth2010autophagyassaysand pages 3-5). This allows the direct discrimination between autophagosomes (yellow) and functional autolysosomes (red-only), providing a critical readout of whether lysosomal degradation is proceeding efficiently or is blocked.

**Measurements** include: (i) quantification of yellow (autophagosome) versus red-only (autolysosome) puncta per cell; (ii) the GFP/RFP fluorescence intensity ratio as a global index of autophagic flux; and (iii) calculation of autophagosome flux rate (J) and transition time (τ) from time-lapse imaging (toit2018measuringautophagosomeflux pages 13-16, dawson2024afluorescentreporter pages 1-3, ueno2020monitoringautophagyflux pages 5-6). These measurements can be obtained via confocal or high-content fluorescence microscopy, flow cytometry, or microplate readers (mishra2018chemicalbiologystrategies pages 5-6). A key advantage is that flux determination does not require lysosomal inhibitors, unlike conventional LC3-II western blot turnover assays (mizushima2020autophagyassaysfor pages 3-5, mizushima2020autophagyassaysfor pages 5-7).

**Cell types** for this assay in a dry AMD context would include ARPE-19 cells (an immortalized RPE cell line already demonstrated to work with the tandem reporter) (govindaraju2017cigarettesmokeinduced pages 2-4), patient-derived iPSC-RPE cells for disease-relevant genotypes, and primary human RPE cells isolated from donor eyes (mitter2014dysregulatedautophagyin pages 14-15). Disease-relevant stressors such as oxidative stress (H₂O₂), cigarette smoke extract, lipofuscin component A2E, or photoreceptor outer segment (POS) feeding can be applied to model AMD-like conditions.

### Biomedical Evidence

Impaired autophagic flux and lysosomal dysfunction occupy a central position in the pathogenesis of dry AMD, supported by extensive biomedical and clinical evidence.

**Autophagy as a core pathogenic mechanism.** The RPE is a post-mitotic, highly phagocytic epithelium that daily internalizes and degrades shed photoreceptor outer segments—a process that imposes an extraordinary burden on the lysosomal network (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 2-4). With aging, autophagic flux declines in RPE cells, either due to reduced processing capacity or the cumulative accumulation of degradation-resistant material (wong2022exploringthepathogenesis pages 7-8). In AMD patients specifically, autophagy proteins and autophagic flux are significantly reduced in RPE cells compared to age-matched controls (wong2022exploringthepathogenesis pages 7-8, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 2-3). When core autophagy genes (ATG5, ATG7) are depleted experimentally, RPE cells develop an AMD-like phenotype including thickening, pigmentary abnormalities, and oxidized protein accumulation (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 2-3).

**Lysosomal dysfunction drives lipofuscin accumulation.** Failed lysosomal processing results in enlarged lysosomes containing undigested waste that accumulates as lipofuscin, an autofluorescent residual body characteristic of AMD RPE (wong2022exploringthepathogenesis pages 7-8). Lipofuscin components such as A2E are refractory to lysosomal hydrolases and, once accumulated, further inhibit lysosomal function by raising lysosomal pH and impairing proton pump activity (si2023theroleof pages 5-7). This creates a pathological feedback loop: lipofuscin accumulation → lysosomal dysfunction → impaired autophagic flux → further accumulation of damaged organelles and toxic aggregates → increased oxidative stress and RPE cell death (si2023theroleof pages 5-7, noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 2-4).

**Block in terminal clearance versus autophagosome formation.** A critical pathogenic distinction in dry AMD is that the defect lies primarily in the terminal degradation step (autophagosome-lysosome fusion and lysosomal processing) rather than in autophagosome formation per se (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 4-5, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5). AMD patient RPE cells show marked accumulation of autophagosomes alongside abnormal mitochondria, with reduced autophagic flux attributable to defective lysosomal degradation (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 4-5). This distinction is precisely what the tandem reporter assay is designed to resolve—making it exceptionally well-aligned with the specific nature of the autophagic defect in dry AMD.

**Downstream consequences.** Impaired autophagy drives multiple downstream pathogenic processes including drusen biogenesis through secretory autophagy (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 4-5, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 1-2), RPE senescence with associated pro-inflammatory secretory phenotype (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5), NLRP3 inflammasome activation through lysosomal membrane permeabilization (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 2-4), and mitochondrial dysfunction with elevated ROS production (kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 3-5, noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 2-4). Mouse models with impaired autophagy recapitulate age-dependent RPE degeneration with pigmentation defects, sub-RPE deposits, and atrophy characteristic of late-stage AMD (wong2022exploringthepathogenesis pages 7-8, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 2-3).

**Clinical relevance.** Current FDA-approved therapies for dry AMD (pegcetacoplan and avacincaptad pegol) target the complement system and only slow geographic atrophy progression without reversing existing damage (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 5-6). Autophagy-targeted therapeutic strategies—including mTORC1 inhibitors (rapamycin), AMPK activators (metformin), NAD+ supplementation, and lysophagy promoters (Urolithin A)—represent an emerging therapeutic frontier that directly addresses the intracellular clearance defect (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 5-6, noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 4-5, si2023theroleof pages 10-12). An assay that can quantify restoration of autophagic flux is therefore directly relevant to this therapeutic pipeline.

### Previous Use

**Drug screening campaigns.** The tandem fluorescent LC3 reporter has been extensively validated for drug discovery applications. A landmark study screened 1,120 FDA-approved compounds using flow cytometry-based quantification of the tandem mCherry-GFP-LC3 reporter, identifying 38 potential autophagy activators and 36 inhibitors, with cardiac glycosides (digoxin, strophanthidin, digoxigenin) validated as autophagy modulators (hansen2011followingautophagystep pages 1-2). A three-dimensional spheroid-based screening platform using mRFP-GFP-LC3 in U343 glioma cells screened 94 natural compounds and discovered nonactin, a cyclic ionophore, as a novel and potent autophagy inducer (pampaloni2017anovelcellular pages 1-3). In C. elegans, a related tandem reporter (GFP::LGG-1::mKate2) was used to screen 154 compounds from an autophagy library, identifying NVP-BGT226 (PI3K/mTOR inhibitor), BAY 11-7082 (NFκB/USP inhibitor), and SRT1720 (SIRT1 activator) as robust autophagy modulators (dawson2024afluorescentreporter pages 16-20). The assay has achieved Z-scores up to 0.91, demonstrating excellent robustness for high-throughput screening (koepke2020animprovedmethod pages 16-16).

**Key scientific discoveries.** CRISPR/Cas9 genome-wide screening using expanded autophagy reporter toolkits, including tandem fluorescent LC3 constructs, identified TMEM41B as a novel autophagy factor required for phagophore maturation, revealing its relationship with VMP1 in autophagosome biogenesis (mishra2018chemicalbiologystrategies pages 5-6). The tandem reporter has also been instrumental in characterizing autophagy kinetics during chemoresistance development, where oscillating autophagic flux was detected across stages of drug resistance (mishra2018chemicalbiologystrategies pages 5-6).

**Use in RPE and AMD research.** The Premo™ Autophagy Tandem Sensor RFP-GFP-LC3B has been directly applied in ARPE-19 cells to measure autophagic flux impairment induced by cigarette smoke extract, a major AMD risk factor (govindaraju2017cigarettesmokeinduced pages 2-4). In vivo, a tandem-tagged CAG-RFP-EGFP-MAP1LC3B reporter mouse model has been used to study basal autophagy across retinal cell types including RPE, photoreceptors, microglia, and astrocytes, demonstrating that ~98.5% of retinal phagosomes are in a mature (RFP-only) state under normal conditions (rao2022monitoringbasalautophagy pages 1-2).

**Summary assessment.** The Tandem Reporter Autophagic Flux Assay is a well-justified and scientifically sound approach for screening therapeutics targeting dry AMD. Its greatest strength lies in its direct alignment with a central and well-validated pathogenic mechanism—the block in autophagic terminal clearance in RPE cells—and its proven ability to discriminate between increased autophagosome formation and impaired lysosomal degradation, which is the specific defect observed in AMD patient RPE (noorbergen2026autophagyandmitophagy pages 4-5, kaarniranta2023autophagyinagerelated pages 2-3, mizushima2020autophagyassaysfor pages 5-7). The assay has demonstrated scalability for high-throughput drug screening with excellent Z-scores (koepke2020animprovedmethod pages 16-16) and has already been applied in RPE cell systems relevant to AMD (govindaraju2017cigarettesmokeinduced pages 2-4, rao2022monitoringbasalautophagy pages 1-2).

However, important limitations must be acknowledged. Lipofuscin autofluorescence in RPE cells—especially in disease-stressed or aged models—overlaps with reporter fluorescence across a broad spectral range (450–700 nm), creating a significant technical confound that requires careful optical optimization and potentially alternative imaging strategies (rao2022monitoringbasalautophagy pages 9-11, rao2022monitoringbasalautophagy pages 1-2). Additionally, the assay captures only one axis of AMD pathology and does not directly report on complement dysregulation, inflammatory signaling, drusen formation, or mitochondrial health (cerniauskas2020complementmodulationreverses pages 1-2, zhao2026autophagyinocular pages 6-7). The biphasic nature of autophagy in AMD—initially upregulated in early disease but declining in advanced stages—adds complexity to data interpretation and therapeutic target selection (si2023theroleof pages 5-7, si2023theroleof pages 9-10).

**Recommendation.** This assay is best positioned as a primary or early secondary screen within a multi-assay cascade for dry AMD drug discovery. It should be complemented by orthogonal assays measuring lysosomal acidification (e.g., LysoSensor), cathepsin D activity, p62/SQSTM1 turnover, POS degradation efficiency, mitochondrial membrane potential, inflammatory cytokine profiling, and RPE cell viability under chronic stress. When embedded in such a cascade—ideally using iPSC-RPE from AMD-risk genotype donors with disease-relevant stressors—this assay provides a mechanistically informative, high-throughput-compatible readout that addresses a critical unmet therapeutic need in dry AMD.
